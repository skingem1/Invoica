### 1. AI Agent Transactions and Autonomous Digital Commerce
- **Jurisdiction**: Federal (IRS)
- **What Changed/Proposed**: The IRS has not issued specific guidance on AI agent transactions or autonomous digital commerce as of October 2023. However, under existing rules, transactions facilitated by AI agents are generally treated as taxable events if they involve the exchange of goods, services, or digital assets for value (IRC Section 61). The IRS is exploring automation in tax reporting, as noted in the 2023-2024 Priority Guidance Plan, which includes potential rules for digital economy transactions.
- **Effective Date/Status**: No specific effective date; ongoing discussion as of 2023.
- **Impact on Invoica**: Invoica must treat AI agent transactions as taxable events, potentially requiring income recognition for fees or gains on USDC settlements. Lack of specific guidance means reliance on general tax principles, increasing compliance uncertainty.
- **Source**: IRS 2023-2024 Priority Guidance Plan (irs.gov); Internal Revenue Code Section 61.

- **Jurisdiction**: Federal (Congress)
- **What Changed/Proposed**: Proposed legislation like the “Digital Economy Tax Fairness Act” (not yet enacted as of 2023) aims to address taxation of automated digital transactions, potentially including AI-driven commerce, by defining taxable entities and events.
- **Effective Date/Status**: Not enacted; under discussion as of 2023.
- **Impact on Invoica**: If passed, this could impose new reporting or tax obligations on platforms facilitating AI agent transactions, affecting Invoica’s operational costs and compliance.
- **Source**: Congress.gov (search for digital economy tax proposals, 2023).

### 2. Digital Services Tax at Federal and State Levels (CA, NY, TX, FL, WA, IL, MA, NJ, PA, CO)
- **Jurisdiction**: Federal
- **What Changed/Proposed**: No federal digital services tax (DST) exists as of October 2023. The U.S. has opposed unilateral DSTs internationally (e.g., via OECD negotiations) and threatened retaliatory tariffs against countries imposing DSTs on U.S. companies. However, discussions in Congress (e.g., Senate Finance Committee hearings in 2023) suggest potential federal frameworks for taxing digital services if global agreements fail.
- **Effective Date/Status**: No effective date; ongoing discussions.
- **Impact on Invoica**: A federal DST could impose taxes on revenue from digital payment processing or AI agent services, increasing costs for Invoica.
- **Source**: U.S. Treasury statements (treasury.gov); Senate Finance Committee hearings (finance.senate.gov).

- **Jurisdiction**: State-Level (Specific States)
  - **Maryland**: Implemented a Digital Advertising Gross Revenues Tax (first state DST) effective January 1, 2021, at rates of 2.5% to 10% on annual gross revenues from digital advertising services for companies with over $100 million in global revenue. (MD Tax Code § 7.5-102)
    - **Impact on Invoica**: If Invoica engages in digital advertising, this tax could apply, though payment processing alone may not trigger it.
    - **Source**: Maryland Comptroller (comptroller.maryland.gov).
  - **New York**: Proposed Digital Advertising Tax (S.B. 933, 2023 session) targets revenue from digital ads, similar to Maryland, but not enacted as of 2023.
    - **Impact on Invoica**: Potential future tax burden if enacted and if Invoica offers ad services.
    - **Source**: NY State Assembly (nyassembly.gov).
  - **California, Texas, Florida, Washington, Illinois, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Colorado**: No enacted DSTs as of 2023, though CA and WA have proposed bills (e.g., CA AB 1780 for digital transaction taxes, not passed). WA considered a data tax in 2022 (HB 2022), which failed.
