### 1. AI Agent Transactions and Autonomous Digital Commerce
- **Jurisdiction:** Federal (IRS)
- **What Changed/Proposed:** There is no specific federal regulation directly addressing AI agent transactions or autonomous digital commerce as of October 2023. However, the IRS treats transactions facilitated by AI agents as taxable events under existing income and sales tax rules if they result in income or the transfer of goods/services. The IRS has indicated interest in emerging technologies via its Strategic Operating Plan (2023-2031), which includes modernizing enforcement for digital transactions.
- **Effective Date/Status:** Ongoing; no specific AI-focused regulation as of 2023.
- **Impact on Invoica:** Invoica must treat AI agent transactions as standard business income or payment processing events, ensuring proper reporting under existing IRS rules (e.g., Form 1099 for payments to vendors). Lack of specific guidance means potential ambiguity in classifying AI-driven autonomous transactions.
- **Source:** IRS Strategic Operating Plan (April 2023), irs.gov.

- **Jurisdiction:** State-Level (General)
- **What Changed/Proposed:** No state has specific regulations for AI agent transactions as of 2023. States generally apply existing sales and use tax rules to digital transactions, which could cover AI-driven commerce if taxable goods/services are involved.
- **Effective Date/Status:** Ongoing; subject to existing sales tax frameworks.
- **Impact on Invoica:** Invoica may need to assess whether AI agent transactions trigger sales tax obligations in states where services are rendered or consumed, especially if acting as a marketplace facilitator (see below).
- **Source:** Multistate Tax Commission (mtc.gov), state tax websites.

### 2. Digital Services Tax (Federal and State: CA, NY, TX, FL, WA, IL, MA, NJ, PA, CO)
- **Jurisdiction:** Federal
- **What Changed/Proposed:** No federal digital services tax (DST) exists as of 2023. Discussions around a federal DST have been tied to international OECD agreements on taxing digital economy giants, but no concrete legislation has passed.
- **Effective Date/Status:** Not enacted; ongoing discussions as of 2023.
- **Impact on Invoica:** No immediate impact, but future federal DST could apply to platforms like Invoica if defined as a digital service provider.
- **Source:** Congress.gov (no specific bill as of 2023); OECD Pillar 1/2 updates.

- **Jurisdiction:** State-Level Examples
  - **Maryland:** Enacted a Digital Advertising Services Tax (first in the US) at 2.5%-10% on gross revenues from digital advertising, effective March 14, 2021. Does not directly apply to payment processing or invoicing platforms.
  - **New York:** Proposed digital advertising tax (S.B. 1124, introduced 2023) targeting large tech companies; not enacted as of 2023.
  - **California, Texas, Florida, Washington, Illinois, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Colorado:** No specific DST enacted as of 2023, though CA and WA have discussed taxing digital services in legislative sessions (e.g., CA AB 1780, 2022, failed). WA imposes a 7% B&O tax on certain digital services, effective since 2020, but not specific to platforms like Invoica.
- **Effective Date/Status:** Maryland (effective 2021); others not enacted or under discussion.
- **Impact on Invoica:** Minimal direct impact unless states expand DST definitions to include payment processing or blockchain-based platforms. Invoica should monitor legislative proposals in NY and CA for broader digital economy taxes.
- **Source:** Maryland Comptroller (comptroller.maryland.gov); state legislative websites (e.g., legiscan.com for NY).
